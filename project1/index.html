Swimming has been around for a very long time, being taught in the Greek and Roman Empire. According to Britannica it was taught in martial training and treated with equal importance to the alphabet in elementary schools. It wasn't until the 19th century that swimming became both a recreational activity and sport. The reason for this was because authorities during the Middle Ages equated swimming with being the cause for epidemics. The first official swimming and diving facility was founded in 1837 in London and consisted of six indoor pools with diving boards. It wasn't until 1846 that the first swim championship was held in Australia. Swimming came to the United States in 1888 when the Amatuer Athletic Union (AAU) officially recognized swimming as a sport. https://www.britannica.com/sports/swimming-sport

Turns or "walls" are one of the most crucial aspects of competitive swimming and can win or lose a race. Coming into the wall, a swimmer must to their best to maintain their speed if not increase it coming into the wall. The importance behind this comes from keeping momentum and adding power to your pushoff. Once you get to the distance that best suits you to flip you should have both of your arms at your side, which you can then use along with you legs to propel yourself into a flipping motion until your legs land on the wall. As your feet hit the wall, you should try to have your feet on the wall for the shortest time possible, to create the most powerful and conservative pushoff you can. As you pushoff you will want your arms to snap into "streamline position", which is when your stack your hands one on top of the other and then place you elbows behind your ears. The tighter your arms are in this position the faster your pushoff will be. After pushing off the wall, you will want to begin your underwater kicks off the wall to upkeep the speed you brought into the wall.

wimming is broken up into four main strokes. With the strokes being broken up into 2 different categories. These categories are long-axis and short-axis strokes, are a called so based on the rythm of the body and rotation of the shoulders. In the long-axis strokes the arms spin on after another streching our the body with a typical kick tempo of 6 kicks for every cycle (equivalent of two strokes). The long-axis strokes are freestyle and backstroke, and the movements between the two are fairly similar aside from the fact that you face the floor in freestyle and the sky in backstroke. Freestyle is the most frequent of all of the strokes as the freestyle event distances range from 50y to 1650y. The other category of strokes is short-axis strokes. This category includes butterfly and breaststroke. Butterfly is the most challenging of all of the strokes however it can reach the highest top speed. In this strokes the body must stay symmetrical in movements as both arms must leave and enter the water at the same time while keeping the legs together in a dolphin kick. Breaststroke somewhat mimics the way that frogs swims and is the most unique of the rest of the strokes. The interesting thing about short-axis strokes though is that they both originally stemmed from the same stroke and over time diverged into dissimilar ways to swim.

When it comes to races, aside from freestyle, each stroke has a 100y and 200y distance race. Along with these races, there are relays in which teams of four can either swim freestyle or have each individual on the team race a different stroke which creates a medley relay. The medley is another event within the swim community for which an individual can race; however, it is called an "individual medley" or "IM" for short. The IM comes in two different distances: the 200y and 400y IM. The order in which the strokes are swam are butterfly, backstroke, breaststroke, and freestyle, and are evenly distributed throughout the race distance.
